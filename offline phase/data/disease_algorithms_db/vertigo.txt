DISEASE: Vertigo

ABBREVIATIONS:
- BPPV: Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo
- CT: Computed Tomography
- IM: Intramuscular
- MRI: Magnetic Resonance Imaging
- TDS: Three times daily
- X-ray: Radiography

DIAGNOSTICS:
- Thorough history
- Clinical examination
- Head thrust test
- Head shaking test
- Dix Hallpick’s test
- Calorie test
- CT brain
- MRI brain
- X-ray neck
- Blood sugar
- Carotid vertebral doppler
- Assessment of nystagmus characteristics (direction, fixation inhibition, vertical/torsional components)
- Presence/absence of tinnitus/deafness
- Presence/absence of associated central nervous system abnormalities

TREATMENT ALGORITHM:

IF the patient presents with vertigo:
    THEN determine if the vertigo is central or peripheral in origin based on clinical features (Table 6).

    IF Peripheral Vertigo features are present (e.g., unidirectional nystagmus inhibited by fixation, tinnitus/deafness often present, no CNS abnormalities):
        THEN consider causes like BPPV, labyrinthitis, vestibular neuritis, Ménière's disease, etc.
        IF BPPV is suspected:
            THEN treat with repositioning maneuvers (e.g., Epley maneuver for posterior canal BPPV).
        IF Ménière's disease is suspected:
            THEN consider diuretics and/or low-sodium diet (1 g/day).
            IF attacks persist:
                THEN consider gentamicin injections into the middle ear or ablative procedures.
                THEN consider Tab. betahistine 24-48 mg/day (initial dose 16 mg TDS initially with maintenance).
        IF Vestibular Migraine is suspected:
            THEN consider antimigrainous drugs for prophylaxis.
            THEN consider antiemetics for acute symptom relief.
        IF Psychosomatic Dizziness/vertigo is suspected:
            THEN consider antianxiety medications and cognitive/behavioral therapy.
        ELSE IF acute vertigo symptoms need short-term control:
            THEN consider pharmacological treatment for symptomatic relief.
            IF Antihistamines are chosen:
                THEN consider Tab. meclizine (25–50 mg 3 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. dimenhydrinate (50 mg 1–2 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. promethazine (25 mg 2–3 times daily, also can be given rectally and IM).
            IF Benzodiazepines are chosen:
                THEN consider Tab. diazepam (2.5 mg 1–3 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. clonazepam (0.25 mg 1 – 3 times daily).
            IF Anticholinergic agents are chosen (for motion sickness):
                THEN consider scopolamine transdermal patch.
    ELSE IF Central Vertigo features are present (e.g., bidirectional nystagmus, no inhibition by fixation, CNS abnormalities present):
        THEN consider causes like vascular events, demyelination, neoplasm.
        THEN investigate further with CT/MRI brain, carotid vertebral doppler.
        THEN manage underlying cause.
        ELSE IF acute vertigo symptoms need short-term control:
            THEN consider pharmacological treatment for symptomatic relief.
            IF Antihistamines are chosen:
                THEN consider Tab. meclizine (25–50 mg 3 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. dimenhydrinate (50 mg 1–2 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. promethazine (25 mg 2–3 times daily, also can be given rectally and IM).
            IF Benzodiazepines are chosen:
                THEN consider Tab. diazepam (2.5 mg 1–3 times daily).
                OR consider Tab. clonazepam (0.25 mg 1 – 3 times daily).
            IF Anticholinergic agents are chosen (for motion sickness):
                THEN consider scopolamine transdermal patch.

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- (None specified in the text for the listed drugs, but standard contraindications apply based on drug class and patient factors).
