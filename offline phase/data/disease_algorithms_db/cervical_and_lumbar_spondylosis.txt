DISEASE: Cervical And Lumbar Spondylosis

ABBREVIATIONS:
- IFT: Inter ferential therapy

DIAGNOSTICS:
- Clinical syndrome resulting from degeneration of intervertebral discs and facet joints.
- Pain and stiffness with decreased range of movement of gradual onset.
- Occasional acute flare ups of pain with muscle spasm.
- Neck pain may radiate to occiput, scapular area and down to one or both arms (may be associated with paraesthesias).
- Back pain is usually diffuse and may radiate up to knees through back of thighs.
- Radiological narrowing of one or more intervertebral spaces, spur formation (osteophytes) and subchondral sclerosis.

TREATMENT ALGORITHM:

# Non-pharmacological treatment

IF acute painful situation THEN
    - Rest.
    - Moist heat in cold weather.
    - Light massage to paraspinal muscles.
    - Cervical traction in the position of maximum comfort to neck (5-10 pounds) for 10-15 minutes.
    - Ultrasonic exposure on painful trigger points in cervical and shoulder muscles.
    - Inter ferential therapy (IFT) for acute neck and back pain.
    - Removable soft cervical collar/back corset/ back belt for symptomatic relief.
    - CAUTION: No exercises.
ELSE IF chronic pain THEN
    - Mobilization and strengthening exercises.
    - Moist heat.
    - Cervical traction.
ENDIF

# Pharmacological treatment

- Same as described for osteoarthritis of knee. (Note: Specific pharmacological treatment details for osteoarthritis of the knee are not provided in the source text).

# Patient education

IF cervical spondylosis THEN
    - Avoid prolonged desk work.
    - If desk work is necessary, take intermittent rest.
    - Use proper writing, typing, sitting posture (avoid low table, use tilt table).
    - Care of the neck.
ENDIF

IF lumbar spondylosis THEN
    - Manage weight (extra weight puts greater stress on back muscles).
    - Maintain proper body alignment while standing with feet slightly apart.
    - When standing for long periods, use a small stool to keep one foot up with knee bent.
    - Do not tense up or concentrate on standing up straight, just stand naturally.
    - Try to avoid carrying heavy bags on shoulders.
    - Care of back while sitting by using a well designed fully adjustable chair to provide plenty of support to the lower back.
    - If chair is uncomfortable use a rolled up towel or small pillow to support the back.
    - When sitting for long periods rest your feet on low stool and use arm rests to support weight of your body.
    - Try not to sit in the same position for long periods.
    - If prolonged sitting is necessary, take rest for a minute or two every hour.
    - To lift weight cautiously by keeping the back as much upright (vertical) and straight (not hunched over) as one can.
    - Do not bend over at waist to pick up the object, instead squat, get under or next to object not on top of it.
    - Lift with your leg muscles rather than back or abdominal muscles.
    - Hold the object close to your body while lifting and carrying.
    - Do not twist your body.
    - To sleep safely on a firm bed.
    - While sleeping supine, legs should be supported with pillows.
    - Do not sleep on your stomach.
    - Sleeping on your side with knees bent and hips tilted forward is probably the best.
ENDIF

# Referral criteria

IF severe pain that disturbs sleep THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF not responding to conservative treatment for more than 2 weeks THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF associated with severe restriction of neck or back movements THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF associated with torticollis THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF associated with sciatic pain THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF associated with constitutional symptoms THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ELSE IF associated with neurological symptoms with back or neck pain THEN
    - Refer to a higher centre.
ENDIF
