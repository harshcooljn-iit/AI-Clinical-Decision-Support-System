DISEASE: Constipation

ABBREVIATIONS:
- UTI: Urinary Tract Infection
- ml: milliliter
- kg: kilogram
- day: daily

DIAGNOSTICS:
- Defined as passage of hard stools difficult to pass, irrespective of frequency.
- Passage of stool less than twice a week is considered constipation.
- Symptoms include fretfulness, poor appetite, intermittent abdominal pain, distension, retentive posturing, history of recurrent UTI, impaired weight gain.

TREATMENT ALGORITHM:

1.  **Initial Assessment:**
    IF Constipation is diagnosed based on symptoms and frequency, THEN proceed to treatment.

2.  **Non-Pharmacological Treatment:**
    IF Constipation is present, THEN implement non-pharmacological measures:
    - Ensure adequate fluid intake.
    - IF infant is not breast-fed, THEN add extra sugar to cow's milk.
    - Add fiber (cereals, pulses, vegetables, salads, fruits, isabgol).
    - Implement toilet training (use bathroom after meals).
    - Maintain evacuation calendar.
    - Use positive reinforcement for successful toileting; avoid punishment.

3.  **Pharmacological Treatment (Initial):**
    IF non-pharmacological measures are insufficient, THEN consider pharmacological options:
    - Option 1: Agarol 2.5-10 ml/day (infants) or 40-90 ml/day (children) in 3-4 divided doses.
    - Option 2: Lactulose 2.5-10 ml/day (infants) or 40-90 ml/day (children) in 3-4 divided doses.
    - Option 3: Mineral oil (liquid paraffin) 5-15 ml/kg/day.
    - Option 4: Milk of magnesia 0.5-3 ml/kg/day.
    - Titrate dose to produce at least one stool/day.

4.  **Severe Cases / Failure of Initial Therapy:**
    IF initial pharmacological therapy fails OR constipation is severe, THEN consider further interventions:
    - Enemas may be used.
    - IF severe cases with faecoliths, THEN enemas may be required daily or on alternate days for initial few days.
    - IF severe cases with faecal soiling, THEN bowel cleaning/disimpaction may be required with enemas 1-2/day or suppositories 1-2/day in initial stages.
    - IF very severe cases with multiple faecoliths, failed medical treatment, mental retardation, THEN surgical disimpaction may be considered.

5.  **Suspected Surgical Cause:**
    IF a surgical cause (e.g., imperforate anus, Hirschsprung's disease, low intestinal obstruction) is suspected, THEN investigate and treat accordingly.

6.  **Patient Education:**
    - Explain the importance of dietary modifications.
    - Do not abandon treatment early after recovery.
    - Bowel training should use only positive reinforcement; avoid negative reinforcement.

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- (None specified in the text for the listed medications, but general contraindications apply based on patient factors not mentioned here).
