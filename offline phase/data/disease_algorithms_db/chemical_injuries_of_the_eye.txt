DISEASE: Chemical Injuries Of The Eye

ABBREVIATIONS:
- SPK: Superficial punctate keratopathy
- AC: Anterior chamber
- IOP: Intraocular pressure
- QID: Four times a day
- BD: Twice a day
- NSAIDs: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs
- EDTA: Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid

DIAGNOSTICS:
- History (time, type of chemical, time to irrigation, irrigation type/duration, eye protection)
- Slit-lamp examination with fluorescein staining (repeat if needed)
- Eyelid eversion (for foreign bodies)
- Evaluation for conjunctival ulcerations/defects
- Check IOP

TREATMENT ALGORITHM:

IF Chemical injury occurs THEN
  Perform Non pharmacological treatment
  Perform Pharmacological treatment
  Consider Surgical treatment if necessary
ENDIF

NON PHARMACOLOGICAL TREATMENT:
  A. Copious irrigation:
    - Place eyelid speculum.
    - Instill topical anesthetic.
    - Irrigate with saline or ringer lactate for at least 30 minutes.
    - IF tap water is the only available solution THEN use tap water.
    - Check pH.
    - IF pH is not neutral THEN repeat irrigation.
    - NEVER use acidic solutions to neutralize alkalis or vice versa.
  B. Double-eversion of the upper eyelid:
    - Perform to identify and remove retained particulate matter in fornices using a cotton tip applicator.
    - IF calcium hydroxide particles are present THEN use a cotton-tipped applicator soaked in disodium ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA).
  C. Debridement:
    - Debride necrosed areas of conjunctiva and cornea to promote healing.
  D. Hospitalization:
    - IF severe injury THEN require hospitalization for regular eye drop instillation.

PHARMACOLOGICAL TREATMENT:
  A. Steroids:
    - Use to reduce inflammation.
    - IF topical steroids are used THEN
      - Use initially (usually 4–8 times daily).
      - Strength depends on injury severity.
      - Taper off after 7–10 days.
    - ALTERNATIVE: Use topical NSAIDs (do not affect keratocyte function).
  B. Cycloplegia:
    - May be used to improve comfort.
  C. Topical Antibiotics:
    - Use for prophylaxis of bacterial infection.
  D. Ascorbic acid:
    - Use to improve wound healing and promote collagen synthesis.
    - Administer topical sodium ascorbate 10% 2-hourly.
    - Administer systemic vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid) 1–2 g QID.
    - IF patient has renal disease THEN do not administer systemic vitamin C.
  E. Tetracyclines:
    - Consider IF significant corneal melting is present.
    - Administer topically (tetracycline ointment QID).
    - Administer systemically (doxycycline 100 mg BD tapering to once daily).
  F. Symblepharon prevention:
    - Prevent by lysis of developing adhesions with a sterile glass rod or damp cotton bud.

SURGICAL TREATMENT:
  - Early surgery may be necessary to promote limbal revascularization, restore limbal cell population, and re-establish fornices.
  - Keratoplasty should be delayed for at least 6 months, preferably longer.
  - Keratoprosthesis may be required for very severely damaged eyes.

GRADING OF SEVERITY:
  - Grade 1: Clear cornea (epithelial damage only), no limbal ischemia (excellent prognosis).
  - Grade 2: Hazy cornea, visible iris details, <1/3 limbal ischemia (good prognosis).
  - Grade 3: Total loss of epithelium, stromal haze obscuring iris, 1/3 to 1/2 limbal ischemia (guarded prognosis).
  - Grade 4: Opaque cornea, >1/2 limbal ischemia (very poor prognosis).

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
  - Systemic vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid): IF patient has renal disease.
