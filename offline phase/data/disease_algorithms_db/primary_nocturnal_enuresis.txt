DISEASE: Primary Nocturnal Enuresis

ABBREVIATIONS:
- ADH: Antidiuretic Hormone
- UTI: Urinary Tract Infection

DIAGNOSTICS:
- Involuntary discharge of urine after the age at which bladder control should have been established (5 years).
- Primary nocturnal enuresis: Child has never been dry at night.
- Secondary nocturnal enuresis: Child has been continent for at least 6 months before wetting again.
- Rule out organic causes (e.g., obstructive uropathy, UTI).

TREATMENT ALGORITHM:

IF Patient age is < 6 years:
  THEN Recommend non-pharmacological treatment.
  - Rule out organic causes.
  - Restrict fluid intake in the evening.
  - Bladder exercises:
    - Hold urine as long as possible during the day.
    - Practice repeated starting and stopping the stream at the toilet bowl.
  - Practice getting up from bed and going to the bathroom at bedtime before sleep.
ELSE IF Patient age is >= 6 years AND non-pharmacological management has failed:
  THEN Consider pharmacological treatment.
  - Option 1: Imipramine
    IF Patient age is 6-8 years:
      THEN Administer imipramine 25 mg once a day at bedtime.
    ELSE IF Patient age is 9-12 years:
      THEN Administer imipramine 50 mg once a day at bedtime.
    ELSE IF Patient age is > 12 years:
      THEN Administer imipramine 75 mg once a day at bedtime.
  - Option 2: Desmopressin acetate (nasal spray, 10 mcg per spray)
    THEN Start with 10 mcg given at bedtime daily.
    THEN Increase gradually by 10 mcg per week to a maximum of 40 mcg per day.
    THEN If effective, use for 3-6 months.

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Imipramine: Not specified in the text.
- Desmopressin acetate: Not specified in the text.

REFERRAL:
IF Organic cause is suspected OR diagnosis is in doubt:
  THEN Refer the patient to a higher centre.

PATIENT EDUCATION:
- Reassure parents that the condition is self-limiting.
- Ask parents to maintain a diary record of dry nights.
- Reward the child for dry nights.
